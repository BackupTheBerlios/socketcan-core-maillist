I know. But with the increasing speed of kernel development we saw quite
a few maintainers trying to decrease their workload. Jean Delvare gave
lm-sensors to Mark Hoffman and was looking for an i2c co-maintainer.
Greg KH dropped PCI maintainership. x86 has three maintainers.

CAN seems to be a very small subsystem at a first glance. But I think
bringing CAN drivers to mainline will be a lot of work for a maintainer,
let alone the maintenance work that'll come up after people started
using these drivers.

AFAICT, there's nobody ATM who will be paid for socketcan maintainer
work. That's why I voted for splitting responsibility between two or three
people. But I agree that it's not our major problem ATM. SocketCAN could
start with one maintainer, we'll soon enough find out if it works or
not.

> Again - this is the model used by many subsytems in the kernel

> And based on other comments on this list is seems
> that driver stuff will be serate from core CAN stuff and that
> core CAN stuff may only be found in -net (and mainline kernel)
> which is again what other subsystems often do - so this is a

My main concern is transparency. If a developer starts work, he should
know against which tree he should build his patches. After the patch was
discussed and accepted on the mailing list, it should be absolutely
clear what happens next, e.g. who will apply the patch to which tree,
and when will it hit mainline. I had some bad experiences with the ARM
tree in the past where an accepted patch disappeared in who-knows-who's
tree until a crippled not-functional version of my patch showed up in
mainline three month later... (I must add that they've improved
their workflow meanwhile)
