On Sun, May 18, 2008 at 06:51:06PM +0200, Sam Ravnborg wrote:
> The maintainer shall more play the role as gatekeeper and competent
> reviewer.

Seconded. And with git, the gatekeeper role is even easier these days,
at least for a project of the size of SocketCAN.

Nevertheless, if the maintainer has pulled patches into his tree, it's
his responsibility to keep it in sync with his upstream, and that's
something which has to be done regularly and needs time.

> > My main concern is transparency. If a developer starts work, he
> > should know against which tree he should build his patches. After
> > the patch was discussed and accepted on the mailing list, it should
> > be absolutely clear what happens next, e.g. who will apply the patch
> > to which tree, and when will it hit mainline. I had some bad
> > experiences with the ARM tree in the past where an accepted patch
> > disappeared in who-knows-who's tree until a crippled not-functional
> > version of my patch showed up in mainline three month later... (I
> > must add that they've improved their workflow meanwhile)
>
> SocketCAN are trivial compared to ARM and thus the processes around it
> can be much simpler. But indeed it is preferable to have transparency
> here.

Yes, but I think Hans-Jürgen is right here that it is important to have
a clear hierarchy of trees. Otherwhise we get the same chaos as with
ARM, where nobody knows any more which tree to work against (it is clear
now, but wasn't in the past).
